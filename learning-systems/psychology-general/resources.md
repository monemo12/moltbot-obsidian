### Day 3-4: Historical Perspectives / ç¬¬ 3â€“4 å¤©ï¼šæ­·å²è§€é»

Psychology emerged as a formal discipline in the late 1800s.

**1. Structuralism (Wundt, Titchener)**
- Goal: Identify the "structure" of consciousness
- Method: Introspection
- Limitation: Too subjective

**2. Functionalism (William James)**
- Goal: Understand the *function* of consciousness (adaptation)
- Influenced by Darwin

**3. Psychoanalysis (Freud)**
- Focus: Unconscious mind, childhood experiences
- Limitation: Hard to test scientifically

**4. Behaviorism (Watson, Skinner)**
- Focus: Observable behavior; environment shapes behavior
- Key: Conditioning, reinforcement

**5. Cognitive Revolution (1960s+)**
- Focus: Mental processes (memory, thinking)
- Mind as information processor

### AI-Generated Summary / AI æ‘˜è¦ï¼ˆè‹±ä¸»ä¸­è¼”ï¼›1 æ®µï¼‰
Psychological research aims to answer questions about behavior using evidence rather than opinion. Researchers translate broad questions into **testable hypotheses** and **operational definitions** (how a variable is measured/manipulated). Common approaches include **descriptive methods** (case studies, naturalistic observation, surveys) for describing behavior, **correlational research** for measuring how variables relate (but **correlation does not imply causation**), and **experiments** for testing causal claims by manipulating an **independent variable (IV)** and measuring a **dependent variable (DV)** while using **random assignment** and control conditions to reduce confounds. Results are interpreted with basic statistics (e.g., effect size, significance) and strengthened by **replication**. Because people can be harmed or misled, research must follow ethics principles such as informed consent (when possible), minimizing risk, confidentiality, and debriefing.
